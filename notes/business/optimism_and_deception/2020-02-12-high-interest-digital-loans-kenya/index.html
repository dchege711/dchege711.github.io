Tala's Version of Events

Empowering female entrepreneurs around the world - Mexico, Philippines, India & Kenya.

69% of global adult population is not covered by credit bureaus. Using non-traditional data, more people can get scored.

Credit brings opportunity and choice. We help people be in control of their finances.

The Situation on the Ground

Tala generates credit ratings from phone data: text messages, location data, contacts, and call logs.

Some messages are extremely valuable, e.g. M-Pesa receipts.

The market is unregulated. Tala's interest rates are ~180% annualized.

In Kenya in 2018, only 8.8% of loans were traditional. 91.2% were digital.

I think this number is inflated. Turns out that banks have also released their own apps, so it's not as if outside apps have dominated the market.

Tala's debt collectors have pressure to meet targets. Thus come the threats of humiliation and manipulation of naive borrowers.

Traditional microfinance, as introduced by Muhammad Yunus's Grameen Bank, targeted small groups of women and used peer pressure to encourage repayment. But it didn't seem to work.

Siroya to the Rescue of Micro-Finance

Making Microfinance Benefit the People and Not the Sharks. Shivani Siroya. https://www.huffpost.com/entry/making-microfinance-benef_b_555361. 2010-06-28.

Intermediary lending orgs must monitor fund usage by partners. They must disavow predatory lending practices. They must explore debt-free alternatives.

Economic development efforts such as microfinance can only be successful if they're alongside community development. [Siroya's first company required entrepreneurs to reinvest in community infrastructure.]

Siroya Takes on the World

Donors were too labor-intensive to find. InVenture was incorporated as a for-profit company. Users would text their income and expenses, InVenture would score them and license this score to banks. Banks weren't that interested.

She pivoted to lending directly - starting with Kenya. Under analysts’ pressure, they charged 15% fee for a 21-day loan (180% annualized).

Borrowers that are late are charged a one-time late fee and barred from taking out another loan.

At least this is a good practice. It might be that additional interest only churns customers while a one-time late fee encourages repeated borrows. But let's assume Tala (and Siroya) has a good heart.

But many borrowers in Kenya took out new loans as soon as they paid off one. Some borrowers spent money on sports-gambling apps. But why would Tala choose slow its growth?

The Math

If only \(75%\) of people pay you back, how much interest do you need to charge?

$$ 0.75 * (1 + r) \ge 1 $$ $$ (1 + r) \ge \frac{4}{3} $$ $$ r \ge 33 \frac{1}{3}% $$

If the rate is actuarily fair and is conveyed clearly to borrowers, then is there a moral issue? If your math says lots of people will default and you choose to go ahead anyway, what does that make you?
