Brian Skinner - how to distinguish good theory from crackpottery

  • Both theorists and crackpots share an irrational self-confidence
  • A crackpot relies primarily on positive evidence
    • Thinks hard about a problem, arrives at a theory that feels right, and then publicizes the result.
  • A theorist incorporates negative evidence:
    • Ponders hard about a problem, arrives at a theory that feels right and then proceeds to try to disprove that theory
    • Takes time to understand competing theories and compare them against her own. If any disagree, she figures out why those theories are wrong.
    • Pushes her theory to the extremes, looks at its limiting cases and checks for disagreement w/ existing knowledge.
    • Only after her theory comes out unscathed does she publicize it.

TEG disagrees with Skinner's emphasis on earnestly trying to disprove a theory

  • Lots of junior colleagues spend far too long making sure they know everything in the given field before saying anything at all.
  • But an earnest well-thought out contribution to the conversation can be useful even if its wrong. Others can save themselves those months of time.
  • Dijkstra's maxim: If you can spend 20 minutes to save each of your readers 1 minute, then it is polite to do so if you expect at least 20 readers.
