I don’t know much about physics but constantly reference Galileo despite knowing little besides the fact that he dared challenge the church.

:-) Dunning–Kruger Effect: a cognitive bias in which people assess their cognitive ability as greater than it is. (en.wikipedia.org)

Masayoshi Son turned $20m into Alibaba into $100b. But he's mistaking luck in VC for his superior intuition. Why invest $300m in a dog-walking app?

I wonder how that deal went through. What in heaven was in their pitch deck? But I heard rumors that America pampers dogs more than children, so ¯\_(ツ)_/¯

Not recognizing your blessings feeds into the dark side of capitalism and meritocracy: the notion that success is a choice, and that those who haven’t achieved success are not unlucky, but unworthy.

I'm relatively lucky. I don't know how much of the techie hubris that I bear. I have unresolved feelings about meritocracy and fairness. Race is usually used as a proxy for bridging the gap, e.g. affirmative action at colleges. But who is affirmative action better suited for - a rich black kid or a poor white kid? I'm in the camp that believes wealth is a bigger divider than race.

Implications

The sectors that have enjoyed the greatest prosperity spread across increasingly few people — technology and finance — have created an unprecedented level of arrogance among people born on third base.

When we feel threatened, we are more prone to see each other as an enemy, rather than someone who has a different opinion. We want to dismiss and fight the whole person, rather than just what they said.

The rich are more generous in times of lesser inequality and less generous when inequality grows more extreme [wired.com, PAYWALL] Why Are Rich People So Mean?.
