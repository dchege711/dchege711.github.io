--- layout: post title: Politics and Rationality date: 2018-08-31 ---

 

 

Politics is the mind killer: "Arguments are soldiers. Once you know which side you're on, you must support all arguments of that side, and attack all the arguments that appear to favor the enemy side; otherwise it's like stabbing your soldiers in the back.

 

How people deal with cognitive bias

  • Change one's view of the facts, e.g. deny that unfair events took place, or edit history to make it appear fair.
  • Change one's mentality - deny that the events are unfair.

Affect Heuristic: When subjective impressions of goodness/badness act as a heuristic, e.g. "a reactor design produces less waste, therefore its probability of meltdown is lower", or "Stalin also says X. You wouldn't want to agree with Stalin, would you?

 

 

 

 

Reversed stupidity is not intelligence. A hundred inventors failing to build flying machines using metal, wood and canvas doesn't imply that what you really need is a flying machine made of bones and flesh. A fool can't be wrong 99.9% of the time, unless they intelligent enough to always pick the wrong answer.

 

Argument screens off authority

  • Given only claims of a flying machine, I'd trust Lord Kelvin (he might know of counterevidence) over Wright Brothers' words.
  • If I see the Wrights' calculations, Lord Kelvin's authority becomes less relevant. If I watch the plane fly, who cares what Lord Kelvin is?

 

Just World Fallacy: Tendency to attribute consequences to a universal force that restores moral balance.

 

 

 

 

Hug the query. Learning about biases may make one forget to examine direct arguments. When its impossible to hug all the queries, at least be aware that you're clinging onto an approximation.

 

Are your enemies innately evil? Everyone is the hero of their own story. It's a more inspiring battle cry to scream 'Die, vicious scum!' instead of 'Die, people who could have been just like me but grew up in a different environment.

 

Correspondence Bias: Tendency to draw inferences on a person's dispositions from behaviors that can be entirely explained by the situations in which they occur, e.g. He's kicking the vending machine - he must be an 'angry person'. Oh, I'm kicking the fucking vending machine because the bus was late and the train was early & the stupid machine has eaten my lunch money for the 2nd time in a row.

 

 

 

 

Fundamental Attribution Error: Tendency to over-attribute others' behavior to their dispositions, while reversing this tendency for ourselves.

 

If you're willing to look at another person's behavior toward you as a reflection of their relationship with themselves rather than a statement about your value as a person, then you will, over a period of time, cease to react at all.
