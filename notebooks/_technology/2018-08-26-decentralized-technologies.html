06/2018: (DSHR) The 4 Most Expensive Words: This Time It Will Be Different

 

The edge of the internet isn't dominated by desktop computers, continuously connected and powered up, with low latency, no bandwidth charges and mostly empty hard disks.

 

Mechanisms needed to prevent cheating among peers are hard to implement in practice or tend to require centralization.

 

Peers would still need to contribute more space than they could receive in return. The replication factor would need to be higher than in a centralized storage network.

 

Storage is a lot less free, thus less of an incentive. Users hate micropayments because they don't take user preferences into account..

 

P2 will be slower than S3, require encryption because of lack of access controls, be less reliable, have volatile pricing, and have a more complex user interface and API because of bidding. P2 can't charge more than S3. And S3 already has notoriously low margins. S3's price has been dropping, so the speculations on P2's price aren't good for providers. And if P2 manages through S3, what prevents Amazon from running P2-on-S3 and capturing the bulk of the market?

 

Economies of scale in P2P networks tend to make the network centralized. Income is linear, but costs are sublinear due to economies of scale. The network will be dominated by a few participants.

 

Like almost all crypto, Filecoin is a way to transfer wealth from later to earlier participants. The VCs paid about $0.80. The earliest buyer paid $1.30. Filecoin is trading at $7.26, down from a peak of $29.59.

 

08/2018: (High Scalability) What Do You Believe Now That You Didn't Five Years Ago?

 

Permissionless anonymity is the primary use case of decentralized systems. Can you trust the likes of Facebook and Google? History says absolutely not, but also says that most people don't care. Two things could make them care:

 

  • A complete deterioration of trust such that avoiding the centralization of power becomes a necessity? China's Digital Dystopia?

 

  • Centralized providers manage to create clearly superior, convenient and reliable applications. Centralization is monetizable. Can decentralized providers catch up despite the resource shortage?

 

 

08/2018: (DSHR) What does the decentralized web need to succeed?

 

A sustainable business model.

 

  • Advertisers, the ones with the money, want to buy access to aggregated data from at most a few suppliers. Users, the ones with the eyeballs, hate ads so much that they've mounted the biggest consumer boycott in history.
  • For more than 20 years, people have unsuccessfully chased the Holy Grail of micro-payments. Bitcoin can't even handle payments. The Lightning Network doesn't work and isn't decentralized.
  • ICOs make customers pre-pay for services they hope to eventually obtain from the startup. This is almost the inverse of sustainable.

 

Anti-Trust Enforcement

 

  • Startups are bought by incumbents before they can become big and profitable. What's there to stop incumbents from buying up the decentralized fellas and killing them off?

 

The Killer App

 

  • It has to be something that users need, but that cannot be implemented by a centralized system. X but decentralized doesn't cut it. Centralized wins.

 

A Way to Remove Content

 

  • Who does the request for content removal go to, and who decides whether the request is valid?
  • At the technical level, it's hard to prevent the abuse of the mechanism for tracking down and deleting all copies of some named content.

 

 

12/2000: (Clay Shirky) The Case Against Micropayments

 

A transaction can't be worth so much as to require a decision but worth so little that that decision is automatic. The anxiety of buying is a permanent feature of micropayments since economic decisions are made on the margin. Beneath a certain price, goods/services become harder to value.

 

Users want predictable and simple pricing. Micropayments waste the user's mental effort in order to conserve cheap resources. Microtransactions assume content, cycles and disk are precious commodities and assume that the user's time is free.

 

PayPal doesn't lower mental transactions nor do they make it easier for users to value a penny's worth of anything. They simply make it possible for users to spend their money once they've decided to.

 

Offline solutions for simple pricing vs cheap-but-not-free are:

 

  • Aggregation. The 'Disneyland' pricing model. Bundles several purchase decisions hence lowering mental transaction costs, e.g. newspapers.
  • Subscription. Producers value predictability: trade lower subscription prices in return for lump sum payments and more predictable revenue streams. Also motivates each party to live up to their part of the bargain.
  • Subsidy. Get someone else to pay. Advertisers subsidize revenue. The weblog movement, the biggest source of subsidy on the net overall, is user subsidized.

 

Micropayment proponents have long suggested that micropayments will work because it would be great if they did. But users want a system that is simple, predictable and easily valued.
