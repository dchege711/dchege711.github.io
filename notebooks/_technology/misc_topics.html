(09/2018: NY Times Op-Ed) The Dangers of DNA Testing

108 crime labs given same DNA mixture and asked to compare it with DNA from 3 suspects (one innocent) from a mock bank robbery.

74 labs wrongly said that the sample included DNA evidence from the 3rd suspect.

If the labs found a match, they were required to report a match statistic that indicates the odds that the match is coincidental or innocent. Among the 108 labs, the match statistics varied over 100-trillion fold.

There are methods (e.g. TrueAllele by Cybergenetics) to reanalyze old DNA mixture data using software that can help analysts correct errors without any new lab testing.

(09/2018: East Bay Biosecurity Group) Your genome isn't private. Maybe it never was.

Discrimination on the basis of genes:

  • China's mandatory whole genome sequencing for selecting Olympic athletes.
  • Laws to prevent prohibitive insurance premiums based on disease risks encoded in DNA.
  • The overhyped 'warrior gene' being used for harsher sentencing based on (weak) predicted aggressiveness.

April 2018: Investigators upload sample from 30-year old rape kit to GEDmatch. Site provides great-great-great grandparents. Investigators then piece out family trees, rough age and location of the suspect. Neither he or any of his immediate relatives had made their DNA public.

Should you have to get consent from relatives before making your DNA public?

Genome-wide mapping of global-to-local genetic effects on human facial shape . Paper predicting facial structure using 38 genetic variants.

Kernighan's Remarks on the Digital Humanities

OCR tends to miss (sometimes) really important context.

Make use of derived data, e.g. age at marriage, gender differences, mortality rates, mapping migrations, last names

Don't spend all your time cleaning data.

Try a sequence of things, not one big idea.

Don't expect existing tools to do the whole job. All the while, building new tools is not as straightforward.

Quantum Computing

Motivations:

  • We're running out of doubling options on IC's for Moore's Law. What's next? Optical? DNA? Quantum?
  • Feynman's lecture on simulating QM. Physicists took the idea and ran with it.

The speedup comes from 2 factors.

  • Super-positioning allows large and probabilistic representation of possibilities.
  • Entanglement of states between qubits at a distance allows us to explore large state spaces faster.

QC can help us solve intractable problems, e.g. protein folding, simulations.

IBM is at 50 qubits. Google has 72 qubits in design. Shor's algorithm for factoring big primes requires more than 10k error corrected qubits. Current error correcting codes need 49 qubits per qubit.
