2015/07: Human Transit: The Transit Ridership Recipe

 

The Ridership vs Coverage Problem

 

Low-ridership and high-coverage systems make little business sense, but may be necessary because some feel entitled to service (e.g. paying taxes) or badly needs the service (e.g. We'll be trapped otherwise)

 

Designing a Ridership Maximizing Transit System

 

The elapsed time between consecutive buses matter. High frequency reduces wait time, makes connections easy, and is a backstop for problems of reliability.

 

As transit ridership goes up, responsiveness to each individual's desires and needs goes down. The big-city subway doesn't take care of your personal needs, but that's inseparable from why it's massively liberating to so many people.

 

Suppose we fix frequency and speed of travel constant. How far do we have to go to be available to a given number of people? We'll consider:

 

  • The number of people, jobs and activities near each stop (denser areas win)
  • Is it possible to walk between the stop and activities around it (grid-like areas win)
  • How direct does the trip feel? (like how you feel bored stopping at Lakeside instead of directly at the Wa)

 

2016/07: Human Transit: Elon Doesn't Understand Geometry

 

Elon Musk's Utopia of Automatic Fleet Managers

 

Traffic congestion would improve due to increased passenger areal density by eliminating the center aisle and putting seats where there are currently entryways, and matching acceleration and braking to other vehicles, thus avoiding the inertial impedance to smooth traffic flow of traditional heavy buses. It would also take people all the way to their destination.

 

Rebuttal: You Forgot About Geometry

 

This increases Vehicle Miles Travelled (VMT). Increasing VMT means your taking more space to move the same number of Engineering can solve increased emissions and road carnage, but not the geometry problem.

 

 

The Dangers of Elite Projection

 

Elites tend to be in positions of power and might be prone to suggest options that work for their predicament. Successful transit is mass transit, and by definition, won't serve the elites.

 

Tech marketing is all about appealing to elite personal tastes on the assumption that whatever we sell to the wealthy today can be sold to the masses tomorrow. But cars in dense cities stop working when everybody buys them.

 

Uber will not make transit obsolete. There's a narrow range of opportunities for ride-sourcing to improve on fixed route transit's efficiency. Furthermore, the increased congestion, emissions and demand for road space are not compensated for in Uber's predatory pricing.
