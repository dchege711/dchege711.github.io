Google's Project Dragonfly

 

  • A censored version of Google search that blacklists websites and search terms about human rights, democracy, religion and peaceful protests.
  • China has 750m people; 80% of its internet access is on Android. There's $$$ to be made.
  • Between 2006 and 2010, Google had maintained a censored Google Search, but was heavily criticized.
  • Google Search was then uncensored because of "Chinese government efforts to limit free speech, block websites and hack Google computer systems"

 

We are Google employees. Google must drop project Dragonfly.

 

  • Dragonfly in China would establish a precedent that makes it harder for Google to deny other countries similar concessions.
  • Providing the Chinese government with ready access to user data, as required by Chinese law, would make Google complicit in oppression and human rights abuses. The Google Search prototype is linked to the user's phone number!

  • FaceTime wasn't efficient in network usage.

Claim: FB will track relationships and social groups and make that information accessible to law enforcement. FB is a corporation that needs to make money; surveillance makes them money.

 

Contention: Actual privacy and security must protect metadata as well as content.

    2018/04 (NY Mag) An Apology for the Internet - How It Went Wrong

     

    1. Start with hippie good intentions
      • Silicon Valley dream was born of the counterculture - a generation of programmers and designers embracing new technology as a tool to transform the world for good.
      • Can Duruk: You were going to Facebook not to work but to make the world more open and connected.
    2. Then mix in capitalism on steroids
      • To transform the world, you first need to take it over. The planetary scale and power turned out to be as well suited for making money as they were for saving the world.
    3. The arrival of Wall Street didn't help
      • Just as FB became the first overnight social-media success, the stock market crashed, sending money-minded investors westward toward the tech industry.
      • Garcia Martinez: It wasn't just the hippies showing up anymore. There was a lot more of the libertarian, screw-the-government ethos, that whole idea of move-fast-break-things-and-damn-the-consequences that still flies under the marketing shell of 'making the world a better place'
    4. And we paid a high price for keeping it free.
      • To avoid charging for the internet - while becoming fabulously rich at the same time - SV turned to digital ads. But to sell ads that target individual users, you need to grow a big audience - and use advancing tech to gather realms of personal data that will enable you to reach them more efficiently.
      • Rich Kyanka: They wanted more people on their platforms. They didn't care if the people were good. They just wanted more bodies with different IP addresses loading up ads.
    5. Everything was designed to be really, really addictive
      • 'Engagement' was the euphemism for the metric, but in practice it evolved into an unprecedented machine for behavior modification.
      • Jaron Lanier: Anger is the emotion most effective at driving 'engagement' - which also makes it, in a market for attention, the most profitable one.
    6. At first, it worked - almost too well
      • None of the companies lied about how their money was made. But as users became deeply enmeshed in the increasingly addictive web of surveillance, the leading digital platforms became wildly popular.
    7. No one from the Valley was held accountable
      • No one seemed interested in bringing such a wealthy, dynamic sector to heel.
      • People don't assign moral agency to algorithms.
    8. Even as social networks became dangerous and toxic
      • User security took a backseat to growth and engagement. Resources went to selling ads, not protecting users from abuse.
    9. And even as they invaded our privacy
      • The more features the platforms added, the more data users willingly, if unwittingly, released to them.
      • There have always been outsiders who criticized the tech industry - even if their concerns have been drowned by the oohs and aahs of consumers, investors, and journalists.
    10. Then came 2016
      • Twitter served as Trump's unprecedented direct-mail-style megaphone
      • Google helped pro-Trump forces target users most susceptible to crass Islamophobia
      • Reddit & 4chan were breeding grounds for the alt-right
      • Facebook was the weapon of choice for Russian trolls and Cambridge Analytica.
    11. Employees are starting to revolt
      • Execs aren't likely to do so. But maybe the employees can rise up and make a change.
      • It became less of a moral positive now - you go to Facebook to write some code and then you go home.
    12. To fix it, we'll need a new business model
      • Maybe by trying something radical and new - like charging users for goods and services
    13. … And some tough regulation
      • Stallman: Fuck them - there's no reason we should let them exist if the price is knowing everything about us.
    14. Maybe nothing will change
      • The behemoths of the new internet are too rich, too powerful and too addictive for anyone to fix
    15. … Unless, at the very least, some new people are in charge
      • Ellen Pao: The internet was a great place to share information and connect with like-minded people. But the people that started these companies were very homogenous.
