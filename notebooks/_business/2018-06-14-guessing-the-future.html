Epsilon Theory: Bayes and the Boreen

 

A Misuse of Bayes: Confirmation Bias

 

When we start with a strong prior and encounter strong evidence, usually a long series of seemingly predictive data, we stop asking more questions. Long term equity returns are always positive. Stocks and bonds are negatively correlated. US residential real estate prices can't fall.

 

We miss that our starting inferences assume other inferences as given. Wars don't happen any more. Globalism and free trade is here to stay. Central Banks have figured out govt indebtedness problems. Markets are mature and liquid.

 

Investors that started their careers in '87, '00 and '08 look at the same data as the rest of us, but their updated priors are stuck in time.

 

Encountering a Boreen

 

When we encounter information that is so afield from any of our priors, we don't know how to incorporate it. What if China really does float the Yuan? Could our internal political divides really manifest in war?

 

 

The Facebooks of the world are few and far between. Unicorns make for good press. Without a desire for fame, an entrepreneur can bootstrap to 2x. 10x is not the only definition of success.

 

Pre-revenue models and exit strategies are not healthy. Essentially passing horseshit to the fool in the market.

 

 

 

Survivorship Bias.

 

Pinning down the cause of a successful startup is hard. Hardworking founders, great culture… what about all the other startups that did all those same things and failed?

 

Isaac Newton made 3 bets: physics, alchemy and theology. In hindsight, the last 2 were a waste of time. But in his time, all 3 fields were equally promising.

 

02/2018: (Housel) Mistaking Trends for Skill

 

Serendipity often masquerades as a skill. Take monetary policy. The top three investing policies are patience, temperament and having your career coincide with a 30-year uninterrupted decline in interest rates.
