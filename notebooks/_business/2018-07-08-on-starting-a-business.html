2013/07: (Paul Graham) Do things that don't scale.

 

  • Recruit manually. Don't send links. Set them up on the spot.
  • Ask how big the startup would be if you did the right things, not whether you're currently changing the world.
  • Delight your initial customers. Tim Cook can't send handwritten notes, but you can.
  • If you can solve the problem manually, do so while gradually automating the most pressing bottlenecks. Saves you from having solutions in search of problems.
  • The Big Launch? Build it and they'll come? Sike!

 

2018/07: Atrium - Why I Love B2B over B2C

 

B2B customers will actually pay for your product. If they want more features, they'll tell you and pay you for it. Consumers are too price sensitive.

B2C's path is fickly viral, e.g. Yik Yak. In B2B, you have a clear execution path. Companies don't quickly flip flop between products. Your product is working if companies are buying it.

B2B customers are rational, and thus understandable. They care about:

  • Does this solution solve my problem?
  • Is it an important enough solution that I will pay for it?

 

Understanding B2C is a different ball game. FB is still tweaking its newsfeed and rotating the content types, e.g. open-graph items, live streams, video, etc.

B2C is a gamble that requires riding a wave, e.g. Google rode the early internet adoption wave that created a ballooning amount of information. The outcomes for B2C generally follow a power law distribution.

 

 

 

2018/07: (HBR) The Average Age of a Successful Startup Founder is 45

 

  • There's a variation among industries: software tends to 40, while oil/gas/biotech tends to 47.
  • Even Gates, Jobs, Bezos, Brin & Page had their growth rates peak when they were middle aged.
  • Different definitions of success, e.g. employment growth, sales growth, IPO/acquisition, came to the same conclusion.
  • VCs may not be looking for growth potential. Younger cash-strapped founders are more likely to cede to VCs at a lower price.
  • Factors at play? Work experience, financial resources, deeper social networks?

     

 

2012/11: (Paul Graham) How to Get Startup Ideas

 

  • Err on the side of having competitors, but make sure that you have a thesis that everyone else is overlooking, e.g. Google wasn't averse to users leaving google.com ASAP
  • In college, take classes in different areas in lieu of taking entrepreneurship classes. Chances are that there are problems ripe for a software approach.
  • Don't try to think of startup ideas. Instead, notice problems that you're facing yourself. Be an expert in that market.
  • Live in the future. Strive to notice what is currently missing, e.g. PG in the 90s noticed that hand-built online stores of custom HTML pages would have to be generated by software soon.
  • Work on projects that seem like they'd be cool. Things that already exist won't seem as interesting to build.
  • Between something that has a large # of people want a small amount and something that a small # of people want a large amount, go for the second.

 

TIL: The Shirky Principle

 

Institutions will try to preserve the problem to which they are the solution, e.g. Turbotax & complex tax system, Taxis vs Ride Sharing, Defense Contractors and never-ending wars, Correction officers vs pot legalization.

 

It's easier for incumbents to prolong the problem than to pivot entirely.

 

 

 

2018/09: (Steve Blank) Is the Lean Startup Dead?

 

Katzenberg (Paramount, Disney Studios & DreamWorks) has raised $1.75b for NewTV, a startup banking on consumers wanting a subscription for 10-minute programs on mobile.

 

After the rubble of the dotcom crash, startups and investors needed a lean approach to survive long enough to generate revenue and profits. "Build it and they'll come" wouldn't cut it.

 

Investors are now looking for the next Ubers and Airbnbs. The valuations are well passed the peak of the 2000 internet bubble. VCs don't have to wait till IPO - they can sell part of their investment when they raise the next round.

 

When capital for startups is readily available at scale, it makes more sense to go big, fast and make mistakes than it does to search for product/market fit.

 

Still, unless your startup has access to large pools of capital or have a brand name like Katzenberg, Lean still makes sense. Lean is now essential for companies and government agencies to deliver innovation at speed.

 

 

2018/02: (Morgan Housel) Why Competitive Edges Die

 

The Beginner's Mind. Being right is the enemy of staying right because it leads you to forget the way the world works. Knowing you have a competitive advantage reduces the incentive to explore other ideas, especially when those ideas conflict with your chosen strategy.

 

The Burden of Being Profitable. Nothing to lose is a wonderful thing to have. Having a quarterly dividend to maintain is what happens after you build something great. But it can come at the expense of what made you successful in the first place.

 

 

2018/08: (Morgan Housel) The Trajectory of Great Ideas

 

Sears. Blockbuster. Nokia. AOL. You had a great idea. You discovered a beautiful beach to lay on, but other people didn't just follow you and lay down themselves; they built condos and restaurants while you were watching the sunset. Soon your great idea loses its appeal. But you put so much work into the great idea that it's become an inseparable part of your identity. Then the world changed - as it always does - but you kept that mentality of conviction and defense. Patience has turned to stubbornness. And now you're the proud owner of a bad idea.

 

 

10/2018 (Myers @CS Colloquia) How Does a Market Work?

 

  • Targets: low latency, high throughput & high reliability.
  • Message formatting:
    • Trader -> NASDAQ using OUCH. 49 bytes only(?)
    • NASDAQ -> all traders using ITCH. 32 bytes.
  • Trading firm listens to broadcasts on buys, sells, deletes on ITCH broadcasts. Online sorting to identify profitable trades.
  • Matching engine operates on a first-come-first-served basis. Earlier orders with better price get traded first.
  • Markets run at 50 microsecond scales.
  • Single market peaks at 10Gbps. Peaks are correlated, e.g. "Shit! TSLA is burning. Sell!"

 

  • Components of a trading system:
    • Quotes: listen to the multicast
    • Strategies: how to decide what to sell
    • Order Entry: different markets have slightly different protocols
